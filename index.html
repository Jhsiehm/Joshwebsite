I. A Disposition · 敦煌

Bearings.

A border is a claim about time as much as territory — a decision cities cannot unmake, written against some clock the mapmaker chose. The institutions I keep returning to are working under that pressure: memory regimes, legislative coalitions, the governance of systems we are still learning to name. They do not resolve; they hold.

An island across a strait whose name two governments contest. A grandmother whose pronunciation will not be standardized. Texts read twice — once for what they say, once for what they had to pass through to get said. Two things that should not both be true usually are. The work is to sit there without flinching.

Adage

慢工出細活 — slow work produces fine craft. A Taiwanese proverb I believe in more than I can usually practice.

Middle Way

The Madhyamaka teaching that things are neither existent nor non-existent is, among other things, an epistemology. It taught me early that the most honest answer is often a third one.

Gloss

Isaiah Berlin's hedgehog knows one big thing; his fox knows many small ones. I am trying to be a fox who reads carefully.

Open Question

Can a civic institution be made legitimate faster than technology can make it obsolete? Unclear. Worth working on.

V. Research & Writing · 拉薩

Scholarship is how I think in public. Three working papers — one book-length in scope — where East Asian memory politics, American state capacity, and comparative governance meet.

2025
Research Article
Folio I · 60+ pp.

Memory After Empire: How South Korea, Taiwan, and Japan Reconstruct Japan's Imperial Past

A comparative analysis of three postwar memory regimes — democratic rupture, postauthoritarian identity-building, and conservative continuity.

Argues that postwar memory regimes emerged not as passive reflections of colonial experience but as active political projects, selectively reconstructing the Japanese past in response to domestic legitimacy struggles. Operationalizes "memory construction" through seven institutional domains — legislative frameworks, education, museum policy, commemoration, diplomacy, heritage, and government-affiliated commissions — applied consistently across South Korea, Taiwan, and Japan.

"South Korea's moralized memory regime derives political force from Japan's reluctance to institutionalize responsibility, while Taiwan's ambivalence is enabled by Japan's diplomatic flexibility. These interactions are reciprocal — memory politics in Northeast Asia are co-constituted."

2025
Historiographical Essay
Folio II · 20+ pp.

Emotional Governance to Bureaucratic Coercion: Reconstructing the Evolution of Japanese Assimilation in Okinawa and Colonial Korea

How Japan's assimilation policy evolved from affective governance in Okinawa to bureaucratic and religious coercion in Korea.

Applies Stoler's framework of affective governance to show how Okinawa served as an ideological laboratory where Japan experimented with shame, loyalty, gratitude, and moral pedagogy as tools of internal colonization — emotional techniques that later hardened into a totalizing administrative system in Korea. Argues Japan's assimilation project was structured around a fundamental paradox: it demanded unity while preserving hierarchy, rendering genuine assimilation impossible.

2024 — 2025
Policy Brief
Folio III · IRB-approved

Strengthening Social Infrastructure to Reduce Senior Isolation

An IRB-approved qualitative study of nine older adults, framing social isolation as a state-capacity failure rather than a personal condition.

By 2034, older adults will outnumber children in the United States — a test of public administration more than of medicine. Using inductive thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke), this brief identifies five dominant patterns driving isolation and proposes five concrete policy interventions: centralized community information, transportation supports, intergenerational program scaling, program diversity, and welcome pathways for newly relocated seniors.

"Seniors are not isolated because they want solitude — they are isolated because the systems around them have not been designed to sustain connection."
